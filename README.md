# Mark Lubin

Research engineer working on cognitive architecture, information dynamics, and adaptive systems.

**[marklubin.me](https://marklubin.me)** · **[synix.dev](https://synix.dev)** · [melubin@pm.me](mailto:melubin@pm.me)

---

My work is concerned with the organization of information in long-lived intelligent systems, and with the ways that organization shapes reasoning, adaptation, and collective behavior across time. I am interested in how systems decide what matters, how they form durable internal structure from experience, how that structure changes as conditions change, and how coherence can be preserved without freezing a system into rigidity.

Much of this began in the practical setting of [agents that persist across sessions](https://github.com/marklubin/kairix), but the underlying questions are broader. They concern not only what a system can retrieve or represent at a given moment, but how it develops, stabilizes, revises, and renews its own organization over longer horizons.

A central thread is the observation that the difficult problems do not usually appear at the level of single-turn competence. They appear when information has to endure, when earlier judgments accumulate consequences, and when a system must continue acting under changing conditions without dissolving into noise or becoming trapped by obsolete structure. In that setting, the important questions are architectural. What should remain explicit and stable, what should be compressed, what should be allowed to decay, what should be reconstructed, and what should be re-evaluated from underlying material rather than merely inherited from prior state? These are not implementation details around an otherwise complete intelligence. They are part of the substance of intelligence in any system meant to persist.

I am especially interested in architectures that treat information not as a flat store but as something differentiated by role, timescale, and consequence. Transient details, working abstractions, durable preferences, procedural traces, and world structure do not behave as one homogeneous class of material. They change at different rates, carry different costs when distorted, and call for different forms of retention, compression, and renewal. The systems that interest me most are not those that merely extend access to more context, but those that can selectively organize experience into forms that remain useful over time. I [read the internals](https://marklubin.me/synix/articles/memory-analysis/) of eight major memory systems to see how the field approaches this. None of them differentiate information by timescale. They all use one flat namespace, one update rate, one retention policy. [LENS](https://github.com/marklubin/lens-benchmark), my benchmark for long-horizon coherence, confirmed it empirically: a SQLite baseline outperformed every purpose-built tool on longitudinal synthesis, I think because the dedicated tools degrade the very information they are meant to preserve.

More generally, I care about the relation between salience, utility, and structure: how systems come to privilege some information, how those decisions reshape later reasoning, and how internal organization emerges from repeated acts of selection. I have been developing a [written treatment](https://marklubin.me/cdr.pdf) of the structural requirements I think follow from this: compression, division, and renewal as properties any persistent system must eventually address.

This has led me toward a broader view of intelligence as a systems problem. I do not think the path to more general intelligence is adequately described as the indefinite enhancement of a single core model. The more interesting frontier, to me, lies in architecture: in the interaction between models, external structure, deterministic processes, learned policies, and mechanisms for revision and coordination. Some tasks call for judgment and inference; many do not. One of the persistent errors in the field is to apply agentic machinery where simple structure would suffice, and then to mistake that excess complexity for depth. The systems I care about use reasoning where reasoning is actually required, and preserve explicit structure where explicit structure is the better tool. What matters is not maximal cleverness at every layer, but the right distribution of intelligence across the whole system.

That view naturally extends beyond the single agent. Once many agents, processes, or human-machine hybrids interact across time, the problem becomes one of collective adaptive structure. At that scale, questions of persistence are inseparable from questions of coordination, specialization, shared state, and institutional memory. I am interested in how higher-order organization emerges in such systems, how local acts of selection and transformation produce durable collective structure, and how open systems maintain coherence without centralizing control. [Aegis](https://github.com/marklubin/aegis-poc) is an early attempt at that. The deeper subject is the dynamics by which information is organized and reorganized within evolving systems, whether those systems are individual agents, multi-agent ensembles, or mixed human-agent collectives.

I pursue these questions through benchmarks, papers, talks, and experimental systems. [LENS](https://github.com/marklubin/lens-benchmark) is one expression of the evaluation work: an attempt to study long-horizon coherence and synthesis rather than limiting judgment to single-turn retrieval or surface recall. [Synix](https://github.com/marklubin/synix) is the main systems environment in which I explore the architectural side: a substrate for experimenting with selective routing, differentiated structure, compression, renewal, and the emergence of higher-order organization over time. Across all of these, the aim is the same: to make the governing dynamics of cognition more legible, more testable, and more architecturally tractable.

Before this, I spent a decade at Amazon building distributed systems across AWS, Prime Video, and payments. That background still shapes the way I think. I am drawn to systems that must survive real constraints, accumulate operational history, and continue functioning under scale, drift, and partial failure. What changed is not an abandonment of systems engineering, but an expansion of it into questions of cognition, persistence, and collective adaptation. The through-line is still architecture. The difference is that the systems in question are no longer only technical infrastructures, but evolving informational organisms of increasing complexity.
